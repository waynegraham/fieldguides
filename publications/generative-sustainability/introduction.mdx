The climate crisis is not merely an environmental challenge; it is a profound threat to our collective memory. As stewards of cultural heritage, libraries, archives, and museums (GLAM) find themselves at the front lines of this transformation.

## Open Source Philosophy

This guide is built upon an open-source philosophy, recognizing that the challenges of climate change are too vast for any single institution to solve in isolation. By sharing our methodologies, technologies, and policies openly, we foster a generative form of sustainability, one that grows and adapts through collective contribution.

Openness is not just a technical choice; it is an ethical imperative. It ensures that the tools for resilience are accessible to all, regardless of institutional wealth or geography.

## Technology & Libraries

The intersection of technology and library infrastructure offers both risk and opportunity. While digital preservation requires energy-intensive data centers, the same technologies can help us monitor environmental conditions, automate metadata creation, and build distributed networks of knowledge that are more resilient to local disasters.

<GuidePullQuote>
  Sustainability in the digital age requires us to rethink our relationship with
  hardware, energy, and the very concept of permanence in a shifting world.
</GuidePullQuote>

We must move beyond defensive preservation, simply trying to hold back the tide, and toward generative sustainability, where our digital ecosystems are designed to be self-healing, energy-efficient, and deeply integrated with the communities they serve.

## A Call to Action

This field guide is intended as a living document. It provides the technical frameworks and policy templates needed to begin this work today. Whether you are managing a small community archive or a large research library, the principles of generative sustainability offer a path forward.
